LEUKEMIA

Leukemia is a type of cancer that affects the blood and bone marrow, where blood cells are produced. It involves the overproduction of abnormal white blood cells, which are the body's primary defense against infection. These abnormal cells crowd out healthy blood cells, leading to various complications.

Types of Leukemia

Leukemia is broadly categorized into four main types based on its progression and the type of blood cell affected: acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), acute myeloid leukemia (AML), and chronic myeloid leukemia (CML).

Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL)

ALL is a rapidly progressing cancer of the white blood cells, commonly affecting children. It involves the overproduction of immature lymphoblasts. Symptoms include fatigue, fever, easy bruising or bleeding, bone pain, and frequent infections. Treatment typically involves chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and sometimes stem cell transplantation.

Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL)

CLL is a slow-growing cancer that affects B lymphocytes, commonly found in older adults. It progresses slowly, and some patients may not require immediate treatment. Symptoms include enlarged lymph nodes, fatigue, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. Treatment options include chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy.

Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML)

AML is a rapidly progressing cancer of the myeloid line of blood cells, affecting adults more often than children. It involves the overproduction of immature myeloid cells. Symptoms include fatigue, fever, easy bruising or bleeding, and bone pain. Treatment involves chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and sometimes stem cell transplantation.

Chronic Myeloid Leukemia (CML)

CML is a slow-growing cancer that affects the myeloid line of blood cells, typically diagnosed in adults. It progresses through chronic, accelerated, and blast crisis phases. Symptoms include fatigue, abdominal discomfort, weight loss, fever, and night sweats. Treatment often involves targeted therapy with tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) and, in some cases, stem cell transplantation.
